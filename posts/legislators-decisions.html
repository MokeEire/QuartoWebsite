Why we need to think about our legislator’s decisions

policy
governance
decision-making
Published

July 14, 2022

Governance institutions around the world appear to be struggling to take on modern challenges as they grow from changes to crises. In the US in particular, legislators have squarely failed to address several ongoing crises. The credibility of the governing institutions is deteriorating as the response to a global pandemic has been delayed and lackluster, the scientific community repeatedly warns of catastrophic environmental risks, and civil society struggles with economic inequality amidst growing corporate influence. Why is the US Congress struggling to pass effective legislation? Can we even evaluate the institutions and individuals who decide the laws we live by?

There is a framework for minimizing decision-making error in individuals and groups known as decision hygiene. In the book Noise, the authors deconstruct the concept of error into two sources - bias and noise - to demonstrate how the solution depends on both. Bias is a consistent influence on the outcome - think the effect of partisanship on a person’s stance on gun policy. Noise describes the inherent variability of the context. For instance, if all we knew was a person’s political affiliation we might predict that a democrat favours gun control and a republican favours gun rights1. But we also know there are democrats and republicans for whom this would be a bad prediction. The many factors we don’t know about that affect a person’s views are what creates the noise.

How would the principles of decision hygiene apply to the decision-making of our legislators? The goal of this article is to answer this question.
